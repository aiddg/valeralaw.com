Understanding Dual Citizenship

Dual citizenship, or dual nationality, means being a citizen of two countries simultaneously. This status offers various benefits, such as the right to live, work, and vote in both countries, access to social services, and the ability to hold two passports. However, it also comes with certain responsibilities and potential complexities, including tax obligations and allegiance requirements.

Valera & Associates PC has particular experience in navigating dual citizenship matters concerning the United States and the Philippines, as well as advising on the implications of holding multiple nationalities in other contexts.

U.S. Position on Dual Nationality

The United States generally permits its citizens to hold dual nationality. U.S. law does not require a U.S. citizen to choose one citizenship over another. A U.S. citizen may acquire foreign citizenship by marriage, or a person naturalized as a U.S. citizen may not lose the citizenship of their country of birth. U.S. citizens, including dual citizens, must use a U.S. passport to enter and leave the United States.

Philippine Dual Citizenship (RA 9225)

The Philippines, through Republic Act No. 9225 (Citizenship Retention and Re-acquisition Act of 2003), allows natural-born Filipinos who have lost their Filipino citizenship by reason of their naturalization as citizens of a foreign country to re-acquire or retain their Filipino citizenship. This also extends to their unmarried children below eighteen (18) years of age, whether legitimate, illegitimate or adopted.

Key aspects of RA 9225 include:

  • Retention/Re-acquisition: Former natural-born Filipinos can take an oath of allegiance to the Republic of the Philippines to become dual citizens.
  • Rights and Privileges: Dual citizens enjoy full civil and political rights and are subject to all attendant liabilities and responsibilities under existing laws of the Philippines.
  • Professional Practice: Those intending to practice their profession in the Philippines shall apply with the proper authority for a license or permit to engage in such practice.

Dual Citizenship FAQs

Can I lose my U.S. citizenship if I become a citizen of another country?

Generally, a U.S. citizen does not automatically lose their citizenship by acquiring foreign citizenship, provided they do so voluntarily and with the intent to remain a U.S. citizen. However, certain actions can lead to loss of U.S. citizenship. It's important to understand these nuances.

What are the benefits of Philippine dual citizenship under RA 9225?

Benefits include the right to travel with a Philippine passport, own land and property, engage in business, and vote in Philippine elections (subject to residency requirements for voting).

Do I have to pay taxes in both countries if I am a dual citizen?

Tax obligations for dual citizens can be complex. U.S. citizens are generally taxed on their worldwide income, regardless of where they reside. You may also have tax obligations in your other country of citizenship. Tax treaties may exist to prevent double taxation. It's advisable to consult with a tax professional specializing in international taxation.
