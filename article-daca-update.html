USCIS Update: I-821D, Consideration of DACA

Published: December 7, 2020 Category: Policy Updates, DACA News Source: USCIS (via Valera & Associates PC)
Home > Legal Insights > USCIS DACA Update (Dec 2020)

Release Date: December 4, 2020 (Effective December 7, 2020)

In compliance with an order of a United States District Court, U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (USCIS) announced on December 4, 2020, that effective December 7, 2020, it is taking the following actions regarding Form I-821D, Consideration of Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals (DACA):

  1. Accepting first-time requests for consideration of deferred action under DACA based on the terms of the DACA policy in effect prior to September 5, 2017, and in accordance with the Court’s December 4, 2020, order;
  2. Accepting DACA renewal requests based on the terms of the DACA policy in effect prior to September 5, 2017, and in accordance with the Court’s December 4, 2020, order;
  3. Accepting applications for advance parole documents based on the terms of the DACA policy prior to September 5, 2017, and in accordance with the Court’s December 4, 2020, order;
  4. Extending one-year grants of deferred action under DACA to two years; and
  5. Extending one-year employment authorization documents (EADs) under DACA to two years.

Further Actions by USCIS

USCIS stated it will take appropriate steps to provide evidence of the one-year extensions of deferred action and employment authorization documents under DACA to individuals who were issued documentation on or after July 28, 2020, with a one-year validity period under the (now defunct) policy memorandum from that date.

The Department of Homeland Security (DHS) indicated it will comply with the court order while it remains in effect, but also noted that DHS may seek relief from the order. This highlights the potentially fluid nature of the DACA policy and the importance of staying informed.

What This Means for DACA Applicants and Recipients

This court order and subsequent USCIS compliance represented a significant development for DACA-eligible individuals and current recipients by reverting to the DACA policy terms that were in place before September 5, 2017. Key implications include:

  • First-Time Applicants: Individuals who met the DACA eligibility criteria but were previously unable to apply due to policy changes could again submit initial DACA requests.
  • Renewals: DACA renewal requests would be processed under the pre-September 2017 guidelines.
  • Advance Parole: The ability to apply for advance parole (permission to travel abroad and return to the U.S.) was reinstated under the prior, more permissive terms.
  • Validity Periods: Both deferred action and employment authorization documents (EADs) granted under DACA would be issued for two-year periods, rather than one year.
"The DACA policy has been subject to numerous legal challenges and policy shifts. It is crucial for individuals affected by DACA to seek qualified legal counsel to understand their rights and options under the current guidelines."
