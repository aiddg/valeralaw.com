Immigration Law

What is the difference between a non-immigrant visa and an immigrant visa (green card)?

A non-immigrant visa allows you to enter the U.S. for a temporary period and for a specific purpose (e.g., tourism, study, temporary work). An immigrant visa (which leads to a Green Card) allows you to live and work permanently in the United States.

For more details, please see our Non-Immigrant Visas and Green Cards & Citizenship pages.

What is "Removal Proceedings" or "Deportation"?

Removal proceedings are formal legal actions taken by the U.S. government to determine if a foreign national should be removed (deported) from the United States. These typically occur in immigration court before an Immigration Judge. Common reasons include visa overstays, criminal convictions, or unlawful entry.

If you are facing removal, it's critical to seek experienced legal representation immediately. Visit our Removal & Deportation Defense page for more information.

How long does the U.S. immigration process typically take?

Processing times for immigration applications vary widely depending on the type of application, the specific USCIS office or consulate handling the case, current backlogs, and the individual circumstances of the case. Some processes may take a few months, while others can take several years. We can provide a more specific estimate during your consultation once we understand your case details.

Anti-Discrimination Law

What types of discrimination do you handle?

We handle various forms of unlawful discrimination, including but not limited to discrimination based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex (including gender identity and sexual orientation), age, and disability. This can occur in areas such as employment, housing, education, and public accommodations.

Learn more on our Anti-Discrimination & Civil Rights page (assuming this page exists or will be created).

What should I do if I believe I've been discriminated against?

If you believe you have been subjected to unlawful discrimination, it's important to document everything: dates, times, locations, individuals involved, specific actions or statements, and any witnesses. Contacting an attorney promptly is also crucial, as there are often strict deadlines (statutes of limitations) for filing discrimination claims.

Are there time limits for filing a discrimination claim?

Yes, there are strict time limits, known as statutes of limitations, for filing discrimination claims. These deadlines vary depending on the type of discrimination and the specific laws that apply (federal, state, or local). It is crucial to consult with an attorney as soon as possible to ensure you do not miss these important deadlines.
