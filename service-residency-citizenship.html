Achieving Your American Dream

Obtaining Lawful Permanent Residency (commonly known as a "Green Card") is a significant step towards building a life in the United States. It grants you the right to live and work permanently in the U.S. and is often a precursor to U.S. Citizenship. The path to a Green Card and subsequent naturalization can be complex, involving detailed applications, stringent eligibility criteria, and often, interviews with immigration officials.

Valera & Associates PC provides experienced legal counsel to individuals and families seeking to secure permanent residency and ultimately, U.S. citizenship. We understand the importance of these milestones and are dedicated to helping you navigate the process with clarity and confidence.

Pathways to Permanent Residency (Green Card)

There are several ways to become a Lawful Permanent Resident, including:

  • Family-Based Petitions: U.S. citizens and lawful permanent residents can sponsor certain eligible relatives for a Green Card. This includes spouses, children, parents, and siblings, each with specific eligibility requirements and preference categories. (See also our Employment & Family Petitions page).
  • Employment-Based Petitions: Foreign nationals with specific job skills, education, or achievements may be eligible for a Green Card through employment. This often involves processes like PERM Labor Certification. (See also our Employment & Family Petitions page).
  • Asylum or Refugee Status: Individuals granted asylum or refugee status may be eligible to apply for a Green Card after one year.
  • Diversity Visa Lottery: An annual program that makes a limited number of immigrant visas available to individuals from countries with historically low rates of immigration to the U.S.
  • Special Immigrant Categories: Including religious workers, certain long-term residents, and others.
  • Adjustment of Status: For eligible individuals already in the United States to apply for a Green Card without having to return to their home country.
  • Consular Processing: For eligible individuals outside the United States to apply for an immigrant visa at a U.S. consulate or embassy abroad.

The Journey to U.S. Citizenship (Naturalization)

Naturalization is the process by which a Lawful Permanent Resident of the United States can become a U.S. citizen. Becoming a U.S. citizen offers numerous benefits, including the right to vote, hold public office, and travel with a U.S. passport.

General eligibility requirements for naturalization typically include:

  • Continuous Residence: A specific period of continuous residence in the U.S. as a Green Card holder (usually 5 years, or 3 years if married to a U.S. citizen).
  • Physical Presence: Being physically present in the U.S. for at least half of the required continuous residence period.
  • Good Moral Character: Demonstrating good moral character.
  • Knowledge of English and U.S. Civics: Passing an English language and U.S. history/government test (with some exceptions).
  • Attachment to the U.S. Constitution: Willingness to support and defend the U.S. Constitution.

Residency & Citizenship FAQs

How long does it take to get a Green Card?

Processing times vary significantly based on the Green Card category, your country of origin, USCIS backlogs, and the specifics of your case. Some family-based categories have long waiting lists due to annual quotas.

Can I travel outside the U.S. while my Green Card application is pending?

Traveling outside the U.S. while an adjustment of status application is pending can be risky and may be considered abandonment of your application unless you have obtained Advance Parole (travel permission) before departing. It's crucial to consult with an attorney before making any international travel plans.

What happens if my naturalization application is denied?

If your N-400 application is denied, you have the right to request a hearing on the denial (Form N-336). An attorney can help you understand the reasons for denial and prepare for the hearing or explore other options.
